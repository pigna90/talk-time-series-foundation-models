Time Series Is Everywhere

You already work with time series

Any measurement recorded over time is a time series. They show up in virtually every industry:

If your data has a timestamp column, you're doing time series analysis — whether you call it that or not.

What do we do with them?

Four core tasks show up again and again across domains:

  • Forecasting — predict future values (e.g. next week's energy demand, next quarter's revenue)
  • Anomaly detection — spot outliers in real time (e.g. fraud, equipment failure, network intrusion)
  • Classification — label a pattern (e.g. is this ECG normal or arrhythmic? is this sensor vibration a bearing fault?)
  • Imputation — fill in missing data (e.g. sensor went offline for 2 hours, reconstruct what happened)

Today, each of these tasks — for each dataset — requires building and training a separate model from scratch. That's about to change.

How We Do It Today

50 years of progress — and the same pain points

The evolution of time series modeling

Classical: ARIMA & friends

The workhorse of statistical forecasting since the 1970s. Models time series as three combined components:

AR
AutoRegressive
Future depends on its own past values
I
Integrated (Differencing)
Remove trends to make data stationary
MA
Moving Average
Correct using past forecast errors
Strengths: interpretable coefficients, fast to fit, well-understood theory, works great for simple stationary data with clear seasonality
Limitations: assumes stationarity, needs domain knowledge (season_length, log transforms), struggles with non-linearity, can't transfer across datasets

Machine Learning & Deep Learning eras

The real pain across all these approaches

Every new dataset = train a new model from scratch

Whether you use ARIMA, XGBoost, or LSTMs, the workflow is the same: collect data, engineer features, select a model, tune hyperparameters, train, evaluate, deploy. Then a new site, a new region, or a new metric comes along — and you repeat the entire process.

To predict the next word well, the model must learn about the world:

Requires geography

The insight: "Predict the next token" sounds trivial. But at scale, it forces the model to build an internal representation of grammar, facts, logic, and common sense — as a side effect of getting better at prediction.

This is the "foundation model" paradigm: train once on a massive general objective, then apply to specific tasks. Can we do the same for time series?

Foundation Models
for Time Series

The concept, the architecture, and the players

The two-stage paradigm

The same idea behind LLMs, applied to time series: learn general patterns first, then choose your path.

"Foundation-al" = one model, many tasks

But how do you feed a time series to a transformer?

Transformers were built for text — sequences of discrete words. Time series are continuous signals with no natural token boundaries.

The key insight

Chronos: Tokenization

Turn continuous values into discrete "bins" — like rounding to the nearest word.

TimesFM: Patching

Group 32 consecutive values into "patches" — like reading word-by-word instead of letter-by-letter. Each patch captures local patterns; attention connects them globally. Same idea as Vision Transformers (ViT).

The paradigm shift

Same backbone, different heads

What practitioners actually say

From Reddit r/MachineLearning, industry conversations, and real-world experience — the skepticism is healthy and often justified:

"My hand-crafted model still wins" — a well-tuned XGBoost or domain-specific model often beats foundation models on specific datasets. Traditional models leverage domain knowledge that FMs lack.
"My data isn't in their training distribution" — proprietary or niche data (internal metrics, specialized sensors) leads to poor zero-shot performance. Unlike text, there's no "Common Crawl" equivalent for time series — training data is fragmented.
"Explainability is a dealbreaker" — many business contexts need interpretable forecasts that can be explained to stakeholders. ARIMA coefficients are transparent; a 200M parameter neural network is a black box.
"Multivariate support is maturing" — many models started univariate-only (MOMENT, Lag-Llama still are). Chronos-2 and TimesFM 2.5 recently added covariate/multivariate support, but cross-variable modeling is still catching up to univariate performance.

Deeper challenges

These are the more fundamental, open research questions:

No universal inductive bias — images all share visual structure (edges, textures). Text all shares language grammar. But time series from different domains (audio waveforms, stock prices, weather sensors) may share nothing in common. Is "time series" even a coherent category for pre-training?
Uncertainty quantification — some models don't provide confidence intervals at all. When they do, it's hard to trace uncertainty back to data quality vs. model limitations vs. inherent randomness.
Data leakage concerns — hard to verify if the FM was trained on the very dataset you're benchmarking on. AirPassengers is the most famous time series in existence — it's almost certainly in every training set.
Computational cost — the largest models (Time-MOE: 2.4B params) require significant resources. Meanwhile, ARIMA runs in milliseconds on a single CPU core. For real-time or edge applications, this matters.

So when should you use them?

Good fit
  • Zero-shot on new datasets — no historical model to start from
  • Rapid prototyping — get a baseline in minutes, not weeks
  • No ML team available — 3 lines of code, no expertise required
  • Diverse forecasting — hundreds of series across different domains
  • Probabilistic forecasts — built-in confidence intervals
Bad fit
  • Known physics — domain equations will beat any black box
  • Need interpretability — stakeholders want to know why
  • Complex multivariate — most FMs are univariate only
  • Edge / real-time — ARIMA runs in ms, FMs need more compute
  • Highly specialized data — far from training distribution

Best strategy today: use FMs as a strong baseline. Compare against your tuned models. Fine-tune the FM if it shows promise. Don't choose — use both.

The counter-argument

For the majority of users still running ARIMA or Prophet, a foundation model is a massive, easy upgrade.

— Nixtla (creators of TimeGPT)

The bar isn't "beat the best ML team with years of domain expertise." It's "beat what most teams actually have in production today" — which is often a basic ARIMA, a Prophet model someone set up two years ago, or a spreadsheet with manual estimates.

For those teams, a foundation model that takes 3 lines of code and produces competitive forecasts with confidence intervals is a genuine step change — even if it's not state-of-the-art on any specific benchmark.

Model size comparison

The complexity of the task determines the model size

Language

Must encode all human knowledge — vocabulary, grammar, facts, reasoning, common sense, nuance across millions of topics and languages. The space of possible text is astronomically large.

→ Requires trillions of parameters

Time Series

Trends, seasonality, cycles, noise, level shifts, regime changes — these are mathematical patterns, not semantic ones. The structure is much more constrained.

→ Hundreds of millions suffice for SOTA

You don't need to "know" that Paris is in France to forecast energy demand.

Nuance: the field is young. Larger models are appearing (Time-MOE: 2.4B params) and it's an open question whether scaling up will help time series as much as it did for language. But for now, state-of-the-art runs on your laptop.

Key Takeaways

Key Takeaways

Pre-train once, use everywhere

The same paradigm shift that transformed NLP and vision has arrived for time series.

Transformers are the backbone

The same architecture behind ChatGPT, adapted for temporal data through patching and tokenization.

We're at an inflection point

Chronos, TimesFM, Moirai, MOMENT — all released in 2024. pip install and go.

They won't replace domain expertise

But they dramatically lower the bar for good-enough forecasts, especially on unfamiliar data.

Best strategy today

Use foundation models as a zero-shot baseline. Compare against tuned models. Fine-tune if promising.
